Autonomy Without Accountability

The case study Automated Active Response Weaponry (ACM, 2018) raises serious concerns about Q Industries’ compliance with professional ethics. Q has moved from passive defence technologies to non-lethal and now potentially lethal autonomous responses. Evaluated against the BCS Code of Conduct, this progression exposes ethical, legal and professional failures.

Public Interest (Clause 1): Facial recognition at protests and autonomous weapons risk breaching privacy rights, human rights law and public safety. Public trust erodes quickly when rights are threatened. Google Glass, for example, faced strong backlash due to privacy concerns (Denning et al., 2014). Unlike current military drones, which require human oversight for lethal action, Q’s proposed systems would remove accountability and increase the likelihood of unlawful harm (Horowitz and Scharre, 2015).

Professional Competence (Clause 2): Q ignored ethical warnings and failed to safeguard against misuse. This breaches competence and integrity obligations and exposes the company to negligence and liability. A similar pattern has appeared in self-driving car development, where overstated readiness and unclear responsibility damaged public confidence (Bonnefon, Shariff & Rahwan, 2016; Stilgoe, 2018).

Relevant Authority (Clause 3): Engineers who resigned and spoke out mirrored Edward Snowden, who prioritised public interest over contractual loyalty (Greenwald, 2014). Both cases show the conflict between legal obligations and professional judgement.

The Profession (Clause 4): Q’s retaliation against whistleblowers risks discrediting the wider profession. The Facebook, Cambridge Analytica scandal showed how misuse of personal data by one organisation damaged trust in technology across society and raised questions about the integrity of computing professionals (Isaak & Hanna, 2018; Susser, Roessler & Nissenbaum, 2019).

Q’s pursuit of lethal autonomy prioritises operational goals over public good, violating the BCS Code. Unlike the fictional “Q Branch” of James Bond, real computing professionals must work within ethical and legal limits to prevent cinematic fantasy from becoming real world harm.

Digital promise or digital fatigue?

In Unit 7, I was asked to produce a literature review that critically evaluates existing research, design, and methodology on a chosen topic. My review examines the positive and negative impacts of digital learning tools on student engagement in higher education since 2020.

Literature Review

Literature Review.pdf

Feedback

1. Knowledge and Understanding

The review presents a comprehensive and well-contextualised understanding of how digital tools have shaped student engagement in higher education post-2020. The use of an established framework (Fredricks et al., 2004) to conceptualise engagement adds academic depth and provides a clear analytical lens across behavioural, cognitive, and emotional dimensions. There is a strong grasp of the diversity of tools discussed—ranging from VLEs and video conferencing to gamification, AI, and immersive technologies. The analysis balances breadth and depth effectively, drawing links between technological features and pedagogical impact. The understanding of how engagement is facilitated or inhibited by these tools is well developed throughout.

2. Criticality

There is a moderate-to-good level of critical engagement with the literature. The review effectively identifies both benefits and drawbacks of digital tools, such as flexibility versus digital fatigue, and motivation versus novelty decay. Contradictions in findings—particularly with gamification—are well noted, and the discussion of short-term versus sustained impact is insightful. However, some sections, especially on individual tools, lean towards thematic description rather than deeper comparative critique. Further analytical depth could be added by evaluating assumptions behind the studies cited and by interrogating how engagement is operationalised across methods.

3. Use of Relevant Sources

The literature base is broad and current, with references ranging from foundational works to contemporary studies (2019–2025). Sources span education, psychology, and digital pedagogy, demonstrating good interdisciplinary awareness. Citation is consistently applied, and key studies are well integrated into the narrative. Some sections—particularly on AI and VR/AR—would benefit from more direct comparison between studies to highlight variation in outcomes or methodological quality. A greater presence of empirical or longitudinal studies would further enhance source variety and depth.

4. Structure and Presentation

The review is logically structured and clearly written. Headings are effectively used to guide the reader through key topics, and the flow between sections is coherent. The inclusion of sections such as “Methodological Critique” and “Gaps and Future Directions” demonstrates a sophisticated understanding of the literature review genre. The language is academic and accessible, with few stylistic or grammatical issues. Paragraphs are mostly well-formed, though a few are overly long and could benefit from segmentation. The presentation is appropriate for postgraduate-level work.

5. Academic Integrity

Referencing adheres to Harvard style throughout and is applied consistently and accurately. The balance between direct quotation, paraphrasing, and synthesis is well maintained. There is no evidence of plagiarism or improper citation, and the author demonstrates a clear understanding of academic conventions regarding source use.
