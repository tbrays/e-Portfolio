Discussion Post: Project Failures Study

Project Failures

Read the Agrawal et al. (2024) paper and then answer the following questions.

Question 1

What are the three most common reasons for project failure?

Poor Communication
Ineffective communication between stakeholders, users and internal teams causes unclear requirements, misaligned expectations and delayed recognition of problems. Agrawal, Walia and Anu (2024) identify weak stakeholder engagement and lack of top management commitment, both rooted in communication breakdowns.

Scope Creep
Uncontrolled expansion of project objectives without evaluating time, cost or risk creates instability. Agrawal, Walia and Anu (2024) link scope creep to poor project selection, weak alignment with organisational strategy and undefined performance measures.

Insufficient Technical Expertise
Teams without the required technical skills fail to deliver reliable solutions. Agrawal, Walia and Anu (2024) cite inadequate training and underdeveloped capabilities as major contributors to this failure.

Question 2

Provide two examples of project failures that support your choices.

The Day Before (2023) – Fntastic
Failed due to communication breakdowns and scope creep. Promoted as an MMO survival game, the final product lacked key features and resembled a limited shooter. The shift in design was not communicated clearly to the public or internally. Features from promotional material were removed or reduced. The game was withdrawn from sale within one week and the studio shut down shortly after (Harrold, 2023).

Bowman Radio System – UK Ministry of Defence
Intended to replace Clansman, the Bowman project suffered delays and cost overruns due to uncontrolled scope expansion. New technical and operational requirements were added without change control. The final product was late, over budget and criticised for being unreliable and complex (National Audit Office, 2006).

Discussion Post: Reply

Within their paper, Agrawal, Walia & Anu (2024) propose a Software Design Error Taxonomy (SDET), for use as a practical tool to enable the early identification of errors within the software development lifecycle. Within this paper, an error is defined as the ‘…root cause of software design faults and failures.’ (Agrawal, Walia & Anu, 2024: 2). Such errors have the potential to lead to faults - ‘the manifestation of an error recorded in a software artifact.’ (Agrawal, Walia & Anu, 2024: 2) - and faults can, in turn, result in a failure: ‘the incorrect execution of a software system…’ (Agrawal, Walia & Anu, 2024: 2).

The early identification of errors can help to avoid the more costly consequences of allowing these to develop into faults and failures (Agrawal, Walia & Anu, 2024, Durham, 2021). Within their literature review, Agrawal, Walia & Anu (2024) highlight three key software design errors that have been commonly reported: Knowledge gap errors, organisational errors, and human cognition errors. These error types can therefore be seen as the three most common reasons for software project failure.

Knowledge gap errors can lead to estimation and analysis failures including underestimation of design time, project management, communication and testing - the IBM System 360 project suffering at the hands of such failures (Milward, 2025). Other failures that could be attributed to knowledge gap errors are skills and competency failures that include poor design, poor implementation and poorly tested solutions (Milward, 2025). The European Space Agency Ariane 5 rocket disaster fell victim to this failure type, wherein testing failed to pick up on a software error from a piece of redundant code left over from previous iterations of the software (Gleick, 1996).

Organisational errors can lead to planning-related failures, including scheduling issues, problems with procurement and attempts to implement large changes too quickly (Milward, 2025), for example the US FAA Advanced Automation System failures (Bar Yam, 2003). Organisational errors can also cause communication and engagement-related failures, including expectation management (Milward, 2025). This was evident in the EDS-BSkyB CRM project, in which the customer (BSkyB) claimed that the supplier (EDS) submitted a bid when they knew they didn’t have the skills or resources to deliver, whereas the supplier claimed that the customer had unrealistic expectations (Collins, 2008).

Human cognition errors, in which stress and fatigue are the root cause, can cause quality-related failures that include a lack of testing and delivery management, which can lead to failures in the use of the system (Agrawal, Walia & Anu, 2024; Milward, 2025). The UK Government Tax Credit online system has seen such failings (Computing, 2008). Additionally, from personal experience, the need to meet deadlines for programming assignments caused quality failures, including the exclusion of features such as a ‘back’ or ‘cancel’ option for navigation on certain command line menus; this resulted in the user having to enter an input even if the option was accidentally selected.

I enjoyed reading your post, the real world examples you used worked well. The Ariane 5 case was a strong choice to show the impact of missed errors in testing. The BSkyB and EDS example also stood out as it shows how badly things can go wrong when there is poor communication between teams and unrealistic goals on both sides.

I thought your point about human cognition errors was interesting too. I do not have much experience with software projects myself, but your personal example helped make that type of error more relatable. It is easy to see how stress or deadline pressure could lead to small mistakes that have a big impact, I see it in a lot of my students work when they are near deadlines.

One thing I wondered was whether some of the errors could fall into more than one category. For example, if someone is not trained properly, that could be seen as both a knowledge gap and an organisational failure and even cause a cognitive failure.

Overall, your post was clear, well supported by examples and easy to understand.

Discussion Post: Factors Affecting User Experience

Factors Affecting User Experience

User emotions play a big role in how people experience digital products, especially the first time they use them. First impressions are powerful, but they often do not reflect how users feel after spending more time with a product.

Gu, Tang and Xue (2023) explored this in their study of the halo effect, where users rate a product’s usability more positively simply because it looks good. They found that this effect is strongest right after first use, but it fades within three days. I agree with their conclusion that this has big implications for user interface evaluation, waiting a few days before collecting feedback can lead to more accurate and realistic responses.

Psychological research backs this up. Clean layouts and limited information help reduce cognitive load, which makes users feel more at ease and in control (Mărcuță and MoldStud Research Team, 2024). When a design looks clean, modern and well organised, users often assume it will be easy to use, even if the underlying functionality has flaws. So early feedback often reflects how the interface looks and feels, rather than how well it actually works.

Visually appealing designs also tend to create positive emotions, which make users more forgiving early on (Thielsch and Hirschfeld, 2019). But there is a downside. When a product looks slick, users expect it to perform well. If it does not, those high expectations can quickly turn into disappointment (Bargas-Avila and Hornbæk, 2019).

That is why I think Gu et al.'s point is useful. Delaying evaluation gives users time to see past the surface and form opinions based on real interaction. Attractive design can cover up problems at first, but it also raises expectations. Letting users settle in before asking for feedback leads to better data and better decisions in user interface research.

Discussion Post: Summary

Factors Affecting User Experience

In my original post, I discussed how first impressions of a user interface can be distorted by visual appeal, drawing on Gu, Tang and Xue’s (2023) research on the halo effect. Their findings showed that users initially rate usability more positively if a product looks good, but this effect fades within a few days. I argued that delaying usability evaluations leads to more accurate feedback, since initial impressions are often based more on aesthetics than actual performance.

Edge (2025) reply pushed me to think more critically about how cognitive load influences that early impression. He introduced the distinction between Intrinsic and Extraneous Cognitive Load from Mărcuță and the MoldStud Research Team (2024), which helped me consider how perceived complexity might affect how users judge aesthetics. For example, if someone expects a task to be difficult, even a clean interface might feel overwhelming. I had not considered that users might be estimating their future cognitive effort when looking at a design for the first time.

Millward (2025) added a practical angle, pointing out that many modern devices replicate familiar interfaces to reduce friction, like smartwatch displays mimicking analogue faces. That raised an important question for me: are design choices based on actual usability, or just what feels mentally comfortable? Combined, both replies made me reconsider how much of user experience is shaped by learned patterns and expectations, not just interface quality. It is not just about delaying feedback, it is also about understanding what shapes user judgement in the first place.
